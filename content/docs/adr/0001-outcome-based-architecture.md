# ADR 0001: Outcome-Based Site Architecture (not Practice-Area Catalog)

**Date:** 2026-06-20  
**Status:** Accepted

## Context

The Stones and Stacks website needed an organizing principle. Two options were on the table:

**Option A (previous prototype):** Organize by practice area — visitors see "AI Automation," "AI Policy and Governance," "Digital Wellness," "Project Management" as four equal navigation categories. Each practice describes itself, and the visitor self-selects.

**Option B (selected):** Organize by client outcome — visitors see three outcome categories (Strategic Velocity, Risk-Ready, High-Performance Culture) as the visible front. The four practice areas are internal scaffolding that the visitor doesn't need to navigate.

## Decision

Adopt Option B — outcome-based architecture.

**Why not Option A:**
- Four practice categories force the visitor to diagnose their own problem and match it to a lane. That puts cognitive load on a stressed decision-maker.
- A catalog structure says "we have four things to sell," not "we solve your problem."
- The founders' goal is unified competence ("we handle anything you throw at us"). A catalog undermines that message.

**Why Option B fits:**
- Leads with what the client actually cares about (outcomes), not how the firm is organized.
- Strategic Velocity functions as the front door — it's the most universally felt pain. Workshop serves as a "try us" entry point inside it.
- Calm messaging (no forced categorization) matches the design tone.
- Practices remain visible enough for credibility without becoming structural.

## Consequences

Positive:
- Visitors understand what S&S delivers in one pass, not four.
- Messaging differentiates from "consulting firm with practice areas" template.
- The firm can add or rearrange practices without breaking the site architecture.

Neutral:
- Workshop moves from its own top-level section into Strategic Velocity. Needs clear UI treatment to still feel prominent.
- "About the Company" moves before "Outcomes" in the page flow — the "who we are" story frames the outcomes.

Negative:
- A senior hiring partner specifically looking for "AI Governance consulting" won't see it as a menu item. Mitigation: Risk-Ready is clearly named as an outcome category, and internal pages/CTAs can still direct to specific practices.
